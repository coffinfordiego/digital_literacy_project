# Stay Safe Online — Checklist (India, Students Edition)

---

## Basic Digital Hygiene

- Use strong, unique passwords for every account. Reusing your social media password for banking is one of the easiest ways to get compromised.
- Turn on two-factor authentication (2FA) for Gmail, Instagram, and banking apps. It takes two minutes and adds a significant layer of protection.
- Never store passwords in notes apps or WhatsApp chats. If someone gets into your phone, they get into everything.

---

## Social Media and Daily Use

- Do not click random links sent through DMs or emails, especially ones that feel urgent or claim you have won something. You have not.
- Keep your profiles private and be deliberate about what strangers can see on your accounts.
- Avoid posting your college ID, phone number, or home address anywhere publicly visible.

---

## Device Safety

- Only install apps from trusted sources like the Play Store or App Store. Websites offering modded or cracked APKs are a common malware delivery method.
- Keep your phone and laptop updated regularly. Those updates contain security patches, not just new features.
- Always use a screen lock combined with fingerprint or face recognition.

---

## UPI and Financial Safety

This section matters more than most people realise.

- Never approve a Collect Request unless you are certain you are expecting money from that person. Here is the rule to remember: receiving money requires no PIN. If you are entering your PIN, you are the one paying.
- Do not share your UPI PIN, OTP, or card details with anyone — including people who claim to be calling from your bank. Legitimate bank staff will never ask for these.
- Be cautious with unknown QR codes. Scammers routinely engineer situations where scanning a code results in you sending money rather than receiving it.
- Stick to official, well-known apps like BHIM, Google Pay, or PhonePe. Avoid lesser-known clones that mimic these interfaces.

---

## College-Specific Risks

- Avoid connecting to random public Wi-Fi networks, whether at cafes or unprotected campus hotspots. Without proper encryption, your data is exposed.
- Be sceptical of anything labelled "free notes," "cracked software," or "exam leaks." These are among the most common ways malware gets onto student devices.
- Always verify internship and job offers independently. If any opportunity asks you to pay money upfront, it is a scam.

---

## If Something Goes Wrong

- Report financial fraud immediately to the National Helpline: **1930**. Speed matters — the sooner you act, the better the chances of recovering funds.
- File a formal complaint at the National Cyber Crime Portal: [https://cybercrime.gov.in](https://cybercrime.gov.in)
- Contact your bank right away to block your cards or freeze your account as needed.

---

## A Few Things Worth Keeping in Mind

Most scams work not by breaking technology, but by exploiting emotion and urgency. If something feels rushed, emotionally charged, or too good to be true, treat that as a warning sign rather than a reason to act fast.

The most effective security habit is simply slowing down before clicking, approving, or sharing anything.
